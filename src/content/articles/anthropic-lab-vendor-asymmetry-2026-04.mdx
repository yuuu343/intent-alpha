## TL;DR

Anthropic's public job board on 2026-04-28 carries **451 open roles**. Sales is
the largest department at **132 (29.3%)**. AI Research & Engineering is **69
(15.3%)**. The ratio is **1.91 to 1, sales over research** — and that figure
understates the commercial weight, because Applied AI (22), Engineering &
Design – Product (20), Marketing & Brand (26), Finance (44), and Legal (14)
all attach to the commercial machine. By contrast, the only departments that
make sense as pure research are AI R&E (69) and a sliver of Compute (11) and
Software Engineering – Infrastructure (21). The headcount silhouette is an
enterprise software vendor with an internal lab, not a research lab with an
applied team.

Two adjacent layers of evidence sharpen the same outline.

**arXiv (200 recent papers in our author/affiliation query).** The largest
single research cluster in the dataset is **evaluation, at 76 papers
(38%)** — most of them third-party studies of Claude, MCP, Constitutional
Classifiers, and Claude Code. Agents is next at 46. The academic field around
Anthropic has moved past "what model can they build" and into "how do we
measure what they shipped." That is the bibliographic shape of an established
vendor, not an emerging lab.

**GitHub (25 public repos under the `anthropics` org).** Total stars on the top
six repos: **`skills` (125,320), `claude-code` (118,781), `claude-cookbooks`
(41,749), `claude-plugins-official` (18,096), `knowledge-work-plugins`
(11,626), `financial-services-plugins` (7,813)**. Every repo in the org is a
product surface, an SDK, a cookbook, an industry plugin pack, or a developer
tool. **There are no research repos in the public org.** Anthropic open-sources
its commercial ecosystem and keeps its research private — the inverse of the
distribution one would expect from a lab.

The three layers, read together, tell one story: **Anthropic is structurally
already what OpenAI became sometime in 2025 (PROV-003)** — an enterprise
software vendor with a serious in-house research function — and the public
framing of "AI safety lab" is a vestigial coat draped over a sales-led org
chart. The vertical-integration moves we documented earlier (PROV-001:
datacenters, capex, colocation counsel) make sense as the *infrastructure
gear* a vendor of this shape needs, not as the moves of a lab. Confidence is
**medium** because hiring shape lags structural change by ~6 months, and
because GAFAM AI groups are not visible in our coverage (see
[/coverage/](/coverage/)) — Anthropic could be the leading enterprise AI
vendor while the largest enterprise AI vendors remain invisible to us. Horizon
12–18 months for the falsification conditions in section 7.

(`5111942008` is the Life Sciences slot), and its own counsel attaching to
healthcare and government work. This is interesting on its own terms, and we
return to it in section 5; for the headcount picture, what matters is that
Beneficial Deployments adds another ~13 roles to the *commercial* side of the
ledger, not the research side.

The infrastructure footprint is consistent with the vendor framing rather
than the lab framing. The 11 Compute roles plus the 21 Software Engineering –
Infrastructure roles plus the explicit datacenter / capex / colocation counsel
postings (the cluster we documented in [PROV-001](/articles/anthropic-compute-vertical-integration-2026-04/))
are the build-out *a vendor of this scale needs* in order to defend
unit economics on multi-year contracts. They make less sense as research
infrastructure: a research lab does not staff infrastructure capex
accounting at director level. A vendor with revenue obligations does.

## arXiv: the field has moved into a measurement phase

The arXiv layer requires a methodological caveat first. The query we ran
combines `all:"Anthropic"` with an explicit OR over fifteen seed
authors — Dario Amodei, Tom Brown, Jared Kaplan, Sam McCandlish, Chris Olah,
Jack Clark, Ben Mann, Catherine Olsson, Nelson Elhage, Saurav Kadavath, Tom
Henighan, Andy Jones, Deep Ganguli, Yuntao Bai, Amanda Askell. arXiv has no
reliable affiliation field, so author-name match is the workable handle. The
query is broad on purpose, and it returns three kinds of paper:

1. Papers actually authored or co-authored by Anthropic researchers.
2. Papers *about* Anthropic products — Claude, MCP, Constitutional Classifiers,
   Claude Code, Claude Skills — written by the broader academic community.
3. Namesake noise — researchers with similar names on unrelated topics
   (string theory, electrophoretic mobility, biofuels) that we discard
   mentally but leave in the dataset.

Categories (1) and (2) carry nearly all the editorial signal, and the *split*
between them is itself a finding: the academic field has crossed from
producing Anthropic's own research output into measuring it. Of the **200
papers in the snapshot**, the topic distribution against our cross-reference
regex is:

| Topic | Count | Share |
|---|---|---|
| Evaluation / benchmarking | 76 | 38% |
| Agents | 46 | 23% |
| Alignment / safety | 33 | 16% |
| Post-training (RLHF / DPO / GRPO / SFT) | 14 | 7% |
| Security engineering | 13 | 6% |
| Interpretability | 10 | 5% |
| Inference / serving | 10 | 5% |
| Multimodal | 9 | 4% |
| Pre-training | 7 | 3% |
| Compute / infra | 1 | 0.5% |
| Data quality | 1 | 0.5% |
| Applied / FDE | 0 | 0% |

The headline finding is the 76-paper evaluation cluster. A non-trivial
portion of those 76 are third-party benchmarks of Anthropic-shipped systems
rather than Anthropic-authored evals. A few representative titles in the
snapshot:

- "Measuring the Permission Gate: A Stress-Test Evaluation of Claude Code's
  Auto Mode"
- "Skilldex: A Package Manager and Registry for Agent Skill Packages with
  Hierarchical Scope-Based Distribution"
- "Trojan-Speak: Bypassing Constitutional Classifiers with No Jailbreak Tax
  via Adversarial Finetuning"
- "A Formal Security Framework for MCP-Based AI Agents: Threat Taxonomy,
  Verification Models, and Defense Mechanisms"
- "Cross-Session Threats in AI Agents: Benchmark, Evaluation, and Algorithms"
- "Does Claude's Constitution Have a Culture?"
- "Judging the Judges: A Systematic Evaluation of Bias Mitigation Strategies
  in LLM-as-a-Judge Pipelines"
- "Evaluating Jailbreaking Vulnerabilities in LLMs Deployed as Assistants for
  Smart Grid Operations: A Benchmark Against NERC Standards"

These are not papers from a research lab announcing what it built. These are
papers from outside investigators measuring, attacking, formalising, and
extending Anthropic-shipped products. That is the bibliographic shape of an
established vendor — the same shape that surrounds AWS, Snowflake, or
Databricks at scale. The community treats Anthropic as a *thing whose
behaviour can be empirically studied*, not as a lab whose next paper is the
news.

The 46-paper agents cluster is the second tell. Many of those papers analyse
Claude-as-agent behaviours, Claude Code workflows, MCP tool use, and Skills
packaging — including the literal Skilldex paper, which proposes an academic
framework around the Skills feature Anthropic ships in `claude-code`. When
the field invents package-management formalisms for your product feature, your
product feature has graduated past "research demo" to "ecosystem primitive."

The 33-paper alignment cluster is closer to what a lab would publish, and we
believe a higher fraction of these are Anthropic-authored or co-authored. The
14 post-training papers (preference learning, reward modeling, rejection-gated
policy optimisation, constitutional finetuning) are the technical area where
Anthropic-internal research is most visible in the public literature. The 10
interpretability papers connect to the long-running circuits / mechanistic
interpretability programme that Chris Olah's group made the public face of
Anthropic's research register. None of these clusters is *small*, but none
of them is large enough to dominate the layer either; in aggregate they are
~57 papers (28%), versus the 76 evaluation papers (38%) that the broader
community is producing about Anthropic's surface.

The most diagnostic single number in the layer is the **zero**. Of 200
papers, **zero** carry our `applied_fde` regex — Forward Deployed Engineering,
Applied AI engineering, deployment engineering, customer engineering. This is
the regex that produced 46 hits on the jobs side. The asymmetry is exact:
Anthropic *hires* for applied/deployment roles aggressively and *publishes*
nothing about them. That is the most direct evidence we have that the lab
register and the vendor register are running on parallel tracks within the
same company. The lab publishes about evaluation, alignment, post-training,
and interpretability. The vendor hires Account Executives and Applied AI
Architects. The lab register feeds the public framing; the vendor register
feeds the headcount.

A reader might object that the zero is a regex artifact. We think it is not.
The applied / deployment vocabulary that fills 46 job titles ("Forward
Deployed," "Applied AI Architect," "Customer Engineer," "Deployment
Engineer") is structurally a *practitioner* vocabulary, not a research
vocabulary, and would not be expected to appear in arXiv titles regardless of
how the underlying work was distributed. The point of the zero is not that
Anthropic is hiding deployment work — it is that the lab register and the
vendor register operate in *different language games*, and the company occupies
both, and the vendor side has more of the headcount.

A second methodological honesty: the snapshot covers ~6 weeks (publication
dates cluster in 2026-Q1 and early Q2, with the most recent in late April),
so this is a recent slice rather than an all-time view. A longer window would
include more interpretability work from earlier years, and more pre-training
work from when Anthropic was building its first Claude generation. The
*present-tense* bibliography, however, is the right basis for a present-tense
hiring inference. The two layers describe what the company looks like *now*.

Several patterns are diagnostic.

**Every repository is a commercial surface.** The product itself
(`claude-code`, `skills`, `claude-desktop-buddy`), the SDK fleet (Python,
TypeScript, Go, Ruby, Java, C#, PHP — seven languages), the cookbooks, the
plugin packs, the CI hooks, the CLI, the package distribution. There are no
research code releases. There are no model weights repositories. There are no
training framework repositories. There are no interpretability tool releases.
The work that is open-sourced is the work that helps customers integrate
Claude into their software stacks. The work that stays internal is, by
implication, the research, the training, and the alignment programme.

This is a deliberate distribution. It mirrors the Stripe / Twilio / Snowflake
playbook far more than it mirrors any AI lab's open-source distribution
(compare Meta-FAIR's Llama / SAM / DINO release pattern, or Mistral's open
weights, or DeepMind's AlphaFold). Anthropic open-sources the *integration
layer*, not the *capability layer*. Customers and integrators can build, and
the moat — the model, the alignment, the training data, the evaluation
infrastructure — stays inside.

**The vertical plugin packs mirror the vertical sales segmentation.** The
GitHub org carries explicit plugin packs for `knowledge-work-plugins` (11.6k
stars), `financial-services-plugins` (7.8k stars), and a `life-sciences`
repository (322 stars). The jobs layer carries Account Executives for
Federal, Life Sciences, Manufacturing & Automotive, Academic Medical Centers,
and Tech. The two layers move together. **A plugin pack is a vertical sales
artifact, not a research artifact** — it exists to give the AE in a customer
meeting something concrete to deploy.

**The seven-language SDK fleet is a standard enterprise-vendor footprint.**
Stripe ships official SDKs for ~10 languages. Twilio ships ~9. Snowflake's
connector ecosystem covers a similar range. A research lab ships, at most, a
Python SDK and ports the rest to community contributors. Anthropic shipping
seven first-party SDKs is a deliberate enterprise-integration investment.
Evidence inside the SDK repos: `anthropic-sdk-typescript` is heavily
auto-generated (the top contributors are `stainless-app[bot]` and
`stainless-bot` from the Stainless SDK auto-generation service, with 386 and
345 commits respectively). This is industrial-scale SDK production, not the
artisanal release of a research group's reference client.

**`skills` at 125,320 stars and `claude-code` at 118,781 stars are the
gravitational centre.** Both are recent (the Skills feature is roughly a
year old in this snapshot, Claude Code roughly two), and both have hit the
top tier of GitHub's star distribution faster than nearly any prior product
release in the AI category. The agent SDKs (`claude-agent-sdk-python` 6,600,
`claude-agent-sdk-typescript` 1,349) and the CI hooks (`claude-code-action`
7,329, `claude-code-base-action` 802) sit underneath as the integration
infrastructure for Claude-Code-as-a-product. **The org's centre of mass is a
developer product, not a research artifact.**

What is *not* in the org is as informative as what is. Compare against what a
peer AI lab's GitHub presence looks like: Meta-FAIR carries Llama weights and
training scripts, model cards, evaluation harnesses, fairness toolkits.
DeepMind carries AlphaFold, the JAX research stacks, and Sonnet (the neural
network library, distinct from the Anthropic model). Mistral carries open
weights and reference implementations. Anthropic's `anthropics` org carries
*none* of that class of artifact. The open-source surface is uniformly
downstream — for integrators, not for researchers. Whatever research code
exists at Anthropic stays inside `anthropic-internal` repositories that
the public org does not see.

A small closing data point on the agent angle. The 7-repo `agents` count in
the cross-reference is concentrated in:
`claude-code` (the agentic product itself), `claude-agent-sdk-python` (the
SDK), `claude-agent-sdk-typescript`, `claude-code-action`, `claude-code-base-action`,
`skills`, and `claude-plugins-official`. The agents *vocabulary* is dominant
in the GitHub layer — far more than in the jobs layer (3 hits) — because the
GitHub surface is where the agent product is exposed to developers. The jobs
side does not need to use the word "agent" because the agentic work is
already concentrated inside Applied AI and the small "Agent Prompts & Evals"
team. This is the same vocabulary mismatch that made the cross-reference
table interesting: agents are everywhere structurally, and almost nowhere in
the hiring nomenclature.

## The three layers told together

Each layer alone is suggestive. Together they snap into one diagram.

**Sales-by-vertical (jobs) → vertical plugin packs (GitHub) → third-party
evaluation (arXiv).** The same vertical pattern propagates through all three
layers. Anthropic hires Account Executives for Life Sciences, Federal
Civilian, Manufacturing & Automotive, and Academic Medical Centers. The
GitHub org carries a `life-sciences` repo, a `financial-services-plugins`
pack, and a `knowledge-work-plugins` pack. The arXiv literature studies
Claude in smart-grid operations (NERC compliance benchmarks),
Claude-Constitution culture, Claude Code permission models, and MCP security
frameworks — i.e., the academic community is asking *vertical-applied*
questions about Claude in real deployment contexts. The whole company is
structurally a vertical-specialised AI platform vendor, and the field is
treating it as one.

**The agent infrastructure crosses all three layers.** Jobs: 3 explicit
"agent" titles plus the 22-person Applied AI department plus the 19 Applied
AI Architect roles plus the small Agent Prompts & Evals team. arXiv: 46
papers, including the Skilldex package-manager formalism. GitHub: 7 of 25
repos, including the two top-tier `claude-code` (118k stars) and `skills`
(125k stars) artifacts plus the agent SDK fleet. Agents are not a thing
Anthropic is *researching*; agents are the *product* Anthropic ships, and
the academic field is now formalising the resulting ecosystem.

**The alignment/safety register is real, and it is parallel to the
commercial register.** Jobs: 23 alignment/safety regex matches, plus 19 in
Safeguards (Trust & Safety), plus 13 "beneficial deployments" titles. That is
roughly 55 roles touching safety, which is non-trivial — it is more than the
22 in Applied AI, more than the 21 in Software Engineering – Infrastructure.
arXiv: 33 alignment papers, with what we believe is a higher
Anthropic-authored share than any other cluster in the snapshot. GitHub:
zero. The alignment programme is staffed and published; it is not
open-sourced. This is the inverse of the agent / product ecosystem (heavily
open-sourced, heavily commercial) and consistent with alignment work being
treated as a research-and-policy programme rather than a commercial surface.

**What the layers *agree* on is that the vendor and lab registers run in
parallel, with the vendor register carrying the headcount majority.** A
single-line summary:

> Anthropic is staffed as an enterprise software vendor (132 Sales, 41+ FDE-shape Applied AI/Architect),
> open-sources its commercial ecosystem (25 product-and-integration repos, 0
> research repos), and publishes research that the academic field now studies
> *empirically* rather than waiting on. The "AI safety lab" framing remains
> truthful but no longer leads on the org chart.

This is the same diagnosis we issued for OpenAI in [PROV-003](/articles/openai-enterprise-pivot-2026-04/),
which found OpenAI had completed the lab → enterprise vendor pivot. The
OpenAI dispatch found Sales-or-equivalent at 134 of 651 (20.6%) and Applied
AI at 87 (13.4%); Anthropic on 2026-04-28 is at 132 of 451 (29.3%) Sales and
22 (4.9%) departmental Applied AI, which on a normalised basis is *further
along* the pivot in Sales share and *less far along* in nominal Applied AI
department size. Both companies converge on the same shape from different
starting points: OpenAI from a research-lab origin that always had a
revenue-generating consumer surface (ChatGPT), Anthropic from a research-lab
origin that built its commercial machine deliberately around enterprise B2B.

The shape — Sales heavy, Applied AI heavy, vertical-segmented, plugin-packaged,
SDK-fleeted — is what a frontier AI company that has crossed into product-led
revenue *looks like* at this point in the cycle. The interesting question is
not whether Anthropic is in this shape; the data shows it is. The interesting
question is what the public framing change will look like when "AI safety
lab" stops being load-bearing.

## What this implies for AI broadly

If the leading "alignment-first" lab is structurally an enterprise vendor,
the ideological-and-market separation that the AI discourse relies on — between
the safety-first labs (Anthropic, DeepMind) and the product-first vendors
(everyone else) — is largely a fiction at the headcount layer. Three
implications follow:

**Frontier labs converge to enterprise vendor shape because that is the
revenue model that supports frontier compute.** Anthropic's parallel
vertical-integration into owned compute (PROV-001) and the OpenAI enterprise
pivot (PROV-003) are not independent. Both are responses to the same
constraint: training a frontier model costs single-digit billions, and the
only revenue base that scales fast enough to amortise that cost without
ceding strategic control is enterprise B2B. Consumer subscriptions can
contribute (OpenAI's ChatGPT business is real), but the multi-year commit
revenue that justifies multi-billion compute capex comes from enterprise.
Hence: enterprise sales force, enterprise verticals, enterprise SDK fleet,
enterprise plugin packs. This shape is the *only* shape compatible with
sustained frontier-scale compute investment, and every lab that aspires to
that scale converges on it.

**The "lab" identity is now a marketing artifact, useful for hiring senior
research talent and managing public perception, but no longer descriptive of
day-to-day allocation.** This is not a criticism. The lab identity is an
asset — it underwrites the recruitment of researchers who would not join a
pure vendor, and it underwrites the regulatory and policy access that
Anthropic uses to shape AI governance. But the underlying allocation has
shifted, and the gap between framing and headcount will widen until the
framing follows the structure or the structure reverts.

**The natural follow-on question is which "lab" goes next.** xAI, Mistral,
and Cohere are at varying earlier points on the same arc. Cohere has already
made the structural bet on agents-as-product (PROV-006). xAI is staffed for a
consumer-and-enterprise dual play but has not yet visibly hired the
vertical-segmented sales force. Mistral's open-weights distribution model is
genuinely different — it could end up as the European version of Hugging
Face rather than as a Vendor of this shape. The pattern we have documented
here may be a phase that *some* labs will pass through and others will skip,
but the labs in the frontier-compute tier will all arrive at it eventually
because the economics demand it.

**What this does to the alignment-research conversation is the more uncomfortable
implication.** The public debate has often treated the alignment community as
a separate institutional sphere from frontier model deployment — a research
programme that, in principle, could critique commercial deployment from
outside. The headcount layer tells us that at Anthropic the alignment
programme is *inside* a vendor, and the vendor's revenue obligations to
Federal, Life Sciences, Manufacturing, and Academic Medical customers are
load-bearing for the alignment programme's continued funding. This does not
make the alignment work less serious; the 33 alignment papers in the snapshot
include genuinely substantive technical contributions, and the 19-person
Safeguards team plus the 23 alignment-coded roles plus the 13 "beneficial
deployments" titles are real headcount aimed at real safety problems. But it
does mean that the alignment register inside Anthropic is institutionally
*subordinate* to the vendor register in the way that any research function
inside a commercial company is subordinate to its commercial function. The
field would do well to stop treating "Anthropic the safety lab" as a mental
model that can carry weight in policy discussions, and to start treating
Anthropic the way it would treat any frontier-model enterprise vendor with a
substantial in-house safety team: a serious actor with real safety
investments, whose safety choices are nevertheless made inside an org chart
where Sales (132) outranks alignment (~55 across all relevant departments) by
more than 2 to 1. The two registers can both be true; only one of them is
load-bearing for the company's existence.

## Reasoning, caveats, and what would change our mind

We weight the inference at **medium** confidence, with horizon **12-18
months**. The reasoning chain is:

1. Sales (132) is the largest single department in a 451-role snapshot.
2. The next several departments by size (Finance, Marketing, Applied AI,
   Engineering & Design – Product) are commercial-machine departments.
3. Pure-research-coded departments (AI R&E, Compute, parts of SE
   Infrastructure) sum to less than Sales alone.
4. The trigram pattern (enterprise account executive, applied ai architect,
   strategic account executive, beneficial deployments AE) is the trigram
   pattern of an enterprise vendor.
5. The verticals named in AE titles (Federal, Life Sciences, Manufacturing,
   Academic Medical, Tech, Startups) match the vertical packs in the GitHub
   org (life-sciences, financial-services-plugins, knowledge-work-plugins).
6. The arXiv layer's 76 evaluation papers are the bibliographic shape of an
   established vendor under measurement, not a lab waiting to publish.
7. The GitHub org's 25 repos are *all* commercial / integration surface; no
   research artifacts.

Each step is observable. The *aggregation* into "Anthropic is structurally
already an enterprise vendor with an internal lab" is the inferential leap,
and it could be wrong in five distinct ways.

**Caveat 1: Sales count is conservatively bounded but interpretively
contested.** "Sales" as a department label includes pre-sales technical
roles, partnership roles, and sales operations roles that some readers would
file under product or strategy. We have not attempted to disaggregate.
Conservative reading: Sales is at least 100 of 451, still ~22%. Aggressive
reading: Sales-aligned headcount is closer to 175 of 451 if you include
Marketing & Brand and revenue-side Finance, putting the Sales+adjacent share
closer to 39%.

**Caveat 2: Research roles are spread across multiple departments.**
Researchers may sit inside Applied AI, Safeguards, or Software Engineering –
Infrastructure depending on the work. Our 69 in AI R&E is a lower bound for
research-coded headcount; the upper bound is harder to estimate without
internal access. Even doubling the figure leaves Sales as the largest single
department.

**Caveat 3: arXiv author-name matching is noisy.** The 200-paper sample
contains a meaningful fraction of namesake noise (papers on string theory,
biofuels, electrophoretic mobility) that should not count. We have not
deduplicated by author affiliation because arXiv does not carry it
reliably; future iterations of this analysis will use Semantic Scholar's
affiliation-resolved author records to clean the layer.

**Caveat 4: GitHub does not show internal research repos.** Anthropic almost
certainly maintains private research repositories with training,
interpretability, and alignment code. Their absence from the public org tells
us about the *open-source distribution policy*, not about the existence or
volume of research work. The inference is that the org has decided research
code is moat-relevant and commercial integration code is not — which is
itself the inference of a vendor.

**Caveat 5: GAFAM AI groups remain invisible to us (see [/coverage/](/coverage/)).**
We do not see Apple, Alphabet, Amazon, Microsoft, or Meta hiring data because
their career sites are bot-protected. Anthropic could be the leading
*independent* enterprise AI vendor while the largest enterprise AI groups
overall sit inside hyperscalers we cannot read. Any "Anthropic vs the
industry" framing should be discounted accordingly. The right framing of this
dispatch is "Anthropic is structurally an enterprise vendor in absolute
terms, regardless of where it ranks against the invisible GAFAM AI groups."

The hypothesis flips to **low** confidence if any of the following hold over
two consecutive snapshots (i.e., the next ~12 months at quarterly cadence):

- **Sales drops below 25% of open roles** without a corresponding revenue
  collapse — would suggest a deliberate pivot back toward a pure-research
  posture.
- **Applied AI / Applied AI Architect / Forward Deployed titles shrink
  absolutely**, especially the vertical-segmented AE roles (Life Sciences,
  Federal, etc.). A vendor with commit revenue does not let those headcounts
  shrink unless it is exiting verticals.
- **Anthropic ships a research-only product without an enterprise wrapper** —
  a fully open-weights frontier release, a major API-free interpretability
  toolkit released for academic use, or equivalent. Would suggest the lab
  register has reasserted commercial-allocation primacy.
- **The vertical plugin packs in the GitHub org are deprecated or unmerged
  for a sustained period.** Plugin packs decay quickly when sales conviction
  decays.
- **A leadership change reframes the company strategy as research-first /
  non-commercial.** This is the discontinuous version of the above.

If none of these hold over two snapshots, the hypothesis remains medium and
our framing — **Anthropic as enterprise vendor with internal lab, not the
inverse** — should be treated as load-bearing context for any further
Anthropic-related dispatch.
